How the transliteration works & what to expect

  • Hebrew (BHS-compatible): begadkephat letters render plosive with dagesh (b g d k p t) and fricative without (ḇ ḡ ḏ ḵ p̄ ṯ); שׁ=š, שׂ=ś; matres lectionis are read as vowels (וֹ=ô, וּ=û, ִי=î, final ָה=â); cantillation marks are stripped. Furtive patach (e.g. רוּחַ → rûaḥ) is handled. Ancient scripts accepted too: Paleo-Hebrew (Phoenician block) and Samaritan input are mapped to square-script letters — being consonantal, they yield a consonants-only transliteration (e.g. 𐤃𐤁𐤓 → dbr).
  • Greek (NA28-compatible): η=ē, ω=ō, θ φ χ ψ ξ=th ph ch ps x; rough breathing → h (ῥ=rh); γ before γ κ ξ χ → n; diphthongs αι ει οι αυ ευ ου … handled; accents are not shown (standard convention).
  • Qamats gadol (ā) vs. qamats qatan (o): resolved authoritatively for every word of the Hebrew Bible using the OpenScriptures Hebrew Bible (WLC) morphology — segolate nouns (חָכְמָה→ḥoḵmâ), the quantifier כָּל־→kol-, and hollow-verb forms (וַיָּמָת→wayyāmoṯ) are all correct, while genuinely long qamats (יָד→yāḏ, לְבָבְךָ→ləḇāḇḵā) stays gadol.
  • The one true limit — homographs: a few words are spelled identically but read two ways depending on sentence context (שָׁמְרָה = šāmərâ “she kept” vs. šomrâ “keep!”). With no sentence to parse, the tool gives the commoner reading; these are theoretically unresolvable by any context-free transliterator.
